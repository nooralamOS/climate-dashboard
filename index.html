Climate Impact Dashboard - Project Overview

Introduction

As the DAFRE climate dashboard team, our project was to investigate some “not so obvious” effects of climate change and ideally inform the general NJ residents through a simple dashboard. For this project, as a team, we split up into multiple subteams to focus on a couple of different main ideas, such as Lyme disease, water contamination, and AQI. After splitting up, we spent a lot of time gathering and cleaning up data on these topics. Some topics required a mix of multiple different datasets, and some just required a lot of cleaning up since the data sets were sometimes up 10s of thousands of lines or even 100s of thousands of lines. We then went into a more exploratory phase where we used coding languages such as Python and R in order to derive meaningful insights from these datasets and hopefully come up with good correlations to show the adverse effects of “rising temperatures” or climate change. Below we mentioned the main topics we worked on for this project and have detailed explanations of the work done for them.

Lyme Disease Analysis

To understand whether Lyme disease is affected by global warming and increased temperatures in New Jersey, we used Lyme disease data from the New Jersey Department of Health (doh.nj.gov). After encountering compatibility issues with several other Lyme disease datasets, this New Jersey-specific, county-wise data provided us with more precise information. Along with this large dataset, we also worked with a monthly dataset, which included one data point averaged over the years 2008 to 2022 for each month, as well as another dataset containing all the monthly average temperatures.

We initially focused on cleaning the datasets, ensuring they were New Jersey-specific, and filtering them to retain metrics that would help us identify meaningful correlations later on.

Key Findings

One interesting thing we found was that, in general, the summer months like June, July, and August were when Lyme disease cases were higher than in other months. However, an interesting observation to note is that in August there is a noticeable dip in Lyme disease cases compared to June and July, even though August can be just as hot. After further research, we found that this decline could be attributed to a combination of factors, including reduced daylight hours affecting tick activity cycles, seasonal changes in tick breeding patterns, and other environmental stressors such as humidity and habitat conditions that may limit tick mobility and host-seeking behavior during this period.

Next, we tried to do more data analytics in order to find correlations between annual Lyme disease cases and annual temperatures. However, finding a consistent correlation proved to be extremely difficult with Lyme disease cases in particular, due to the complex behavior patterns of ticks, their sensitivity to multiple environmental factors beyond temperature alone (like humidity, vegetation, and host availability), and geographical differences even within a small state like New Jersey that could cause significant inconsistencies in the data. To still explore the effect of temperature on Lyme disease cases over the years we had available, we normalized the number of Lyme disease cases and the temperatures to better observe any trends. We also decided to exclude years after 2019, since COVID tended to cause a lot of discrepancies in the results. In the graph below, the correlation ended up being around 0.305, indicating that there might not be a direct correlation. However, if we had data on Lyme disease cases from the 1900s, the trends could have been much clearer, but due to limitations in available datasets as well as the complexities that follow the study of ticks and Lyme disease, there wasn’t an exact correlation indicating a direct effect of temperature on Lyme disease.

Water Contamination

Methodology

The analysis leveraged R to investigate relationships between environmental variables (temperature, rainfall) and incidents (floods, nitrate levels) in New Jersey. Datasets from FEMA (flood claims), historical temperature records (1977–2024), and nitrate measurements (2009–2024) were processed using dplyr and lubridate for aggregation (daily, monthly, yearly). Statistical models, including Poisson and Negative Binomial regressions, identified seasonal trends, while correlation analyses (Pearson, Spearman) quantified associations. Data normalization enabled direct comparison of variables like temperature and flood 'incidents. Visualizations (ggplot2, gridExtra) highlighted temporal patterns and regional disparities. County-level mappings and hurricane-linked September data were prioritized to uncover localized climate impacts.

Results

1. Strong Localized Correlations Between Temperature and Floods

While the global correlation between temperature and flood incidents was weak (r = 0.179), Sussex County emerged as a hotspot with a striking positive correlation (r = 0.427), likely due to its topography, where warmer temperatures accelerate snowmelt in mountainous areas, increasing riverine flooding. Similarly, Bergen County (r = 0.303) showed heightened flood risks in urbanized zones, where heat islands intensify stormwater runoff during heavy rains. These localized trends underscore the importance of regional climate resilience strategies, particularly in areas where temperature amplifies hydrological risks.

2. Seasonal Peaks: Hurricanes and September Floods

September exhibited a unique positive relationship between temperature and flood incidents. Regression models revealed a +0.155 Poisson coefficient for temperature, escalating to +0.265 for late-month dates (24th–28th). This aligns with historical hurricane patterns, where warmer Atlantic waters in late September fuel storm intensity, disproportionately affecting coastal counties like Ocean and Monmouth. The late-month spike in incidents correlates with peak hurricane seasons, suggesting that rising temperatures could exacerbate autumn flood risks in vulnerable coastal regions.

3. Policy-Relevant Insights from Positive Trends

The analysis identified actionable patterns:

  • Hunterdon County (r = 0.305), with its riverine networks, faces increased flood risks from temperature-driven snowmelt, necessitating upstream water management.
  • Urban areas like Bergen require upgraded drainage systems to counter heat-induced runoff.
  • September’s temperature-flood link calls for preemptive hurricane preparedness in coastal zones.

These findings highlight the need for adaptive infrastructure and targeted climate policies in high-correlation regions, where temperature acts as a catalyst for environmental hazards.

AQI

Methodology

We used the air quality data from epa.gov, and correlated different measures from it with average annual temperature. Measures included:

  • Avg. Max AQI
  • Avg. Median AQI
  • Avg Number of Good Days
  • Avg Number of Bad Days

The analysis leveraged R to investigate relationships between environmental variables (temperature, rainfall) and incidents (floods, nitrate levels) in New Jersey. Datasets from FEMA (flood claims), historical temperature records (1977–2024), and nitrate measurements (2009–2024) were processed using dplyr and lubridate for aggregation (daily, monthly, yearly). Data normalization enabled direct comparison of variables like temperature and flood incidents. Visualizations (ggplot2, gridExtra) highlighted temporal patterns and regional disparities. County-level mappings and hurricane-linked September data were prioritized to uncover localized climate impacts.

The time frame we chose was [2000, 2018]. We selected this period because the AQI dataset was only complete starting in 1996, so we decided on 2000 as the standard year to begin our analysis. We chose 2018 as the last year of analysis in order to achieve more general results, as the years 2019-2022 are considered abnormal, particularly regarding pollution metrics, due to the Covid-19 pandemic.

To clean the data, we first narrowed the dataset down to New Jersey only, then we took the average across all counties to get an average for NJ. Additionally, we used the same temperature dataset from the Lyme disease analysis, following the same data cleaning procedure. NOTE: All values were normalized for graphing and correlation testing.
